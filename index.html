How the simulation works

1 · Motion: vector addition every 2 seconds

The boat is a point mass integrated with a simple time step (dt = 2 s). Its velocity over the ground is the sum of two vectors: the velocity through the water (pointed along the heading = wind direction ± tacking angle) and the local current. The tacking angle and boat speed come from the selected boat's upwind polar, linearly interpolated at the entered wind speed, and stay constant for the whole run.

V_ground = V_boat(heading, through water) + V_current(x, y) position(t+dt) = position(t) + V_ground · dt

2 · The current field: interpolation between your 4 measurements

The measured currents (start, mark, left and right layline) are blended into a continuous field by inverse-distance weighting: at any position, nearby measurements dominate, distant ones fade out with the square of the distance. Points you leave blank are treated as not measured and dropped from the sum — so with only two points filled, the whole field is interpolated from those two, never from a false zero. With no points measured at all, the field is simply zero everywhere.

V_current(P) = Σ wᵢ·Vᵢ / Σ wᵢ   where   wᵢ = 1 / dᵢ²

3 · The tack decision: current-corrected laylines

At every step the model asks: if I tacked right now, would my course over ground on the other tack point at the mark? It computes the opposite tack's ground vector (boat vector on the other heading + local current) and compares its direction with the bearing to the mark. When the two align — the sign of the angle between them flips — the boat tacks.

tack when: angle( V_ground_opposite , bearing to mark ) crosses 0

Direct sailing (fetch mode). In parallel, the model checks whether the mark is reachable without beating: it solves for the current-compensated heading whose ground velocity points straight at the mark, and if that heading lies outside the no-go zone (≥ TWA off the wind), the boat steers it directly. This mode takes over exactly where the direct heading exits the no-go zone — i.e. on the current-corrected layline — so the final leg is a continuously corrected curve that always converges on the mark, recovering any drift-induced overstand along the way. A run where even this cannot make progress (current stronger than the boat) is reported as unreachable.

4 · The six runs

Each scenario–side pair is simulated independently: 3 wind directions (left phase, mean, right phase — each held constant for the whole run) × 2 strategies (left side = starboard tack first, right side = port tack first). All runs start from the middle of the line. A run ends when the boat is within 18 m of the mark (the final metres are sailed in fetch mode, homing on the mark); the elapsed times feed the results table.

Two guards keep the integration honest: after a tack the boat must sail at least 20 s before tacking again (prevents oscillation on the layline), and a run is abandoned after 4 simulated hours (reported as unreachable — typically when the current overpowers the boat). The chart frame and the four current measurement points are fixed to the start→mark course axis, so changing the wind direction moves the trajectories but never the course, the frame, or the current field.

What the simulation does — and does not — account for

Accounted for

  • Current as a vector, everywhere on the course. Ground velocity = boat velocity through the water + local current. Head current slows progress towards the mark, favourable current speeds it up, and cross current pushes the boat sideways.
  • Spatially varying current. The field is interpolated between your 4 measurement points (start, mark, left/right layline) by inverse-distance weighting, so each side of the course can have genuinely different water.
  • Current-corrected laylines. The boat tacks when the course over ground of the opposite tack fetches the mark — not the geometric layline. The dashed grey lines are shown only as a no-current reference for comparison.
  • Wind direction scenarios. Each run uses a fixed wind direction: left phase, mean, or right phase. Comparing the three shows how sensitive the side choice is to where the wind sits in its oscillation.
  • Boat performance from an upwind polar. The tacking angle and boat speed are read from the selected boat's polar table, linearly interpolated at the entered wind speed. Within a run they are constant.
  • Fetch/reach sailing on the final approach. When the mark no longer requires beating, the boat steers a current-compensated heading straight at it — so it always closes on the mark, recovers small overstands, and effectively tacks exactly on the current-corrected layline.
  • Start line geometry. Line length and its orientation square to the start→mark axis; all runs start from the middle of the line. A line skewed relative to the wind therefore shows up as line bias in the geometry.

Not accounted for

  • Current wind (tidal wind). In reality the boat sails in the wind relative to the water, i.e. true wind minus the current vector. A 0.5–1 kn current can rotate and change the effective wind by a few degrees / tenths of a knot. Here the boat holds its angle to the true (over-ground) wind.
  • Wind oscillation during a run. The wind direction is constant within each run — the boat does not gain from tacking on shifts. Phases are compared as separate static scenarios, not as a time series.
  • Wind speed variation and sailing modes. Wind speed sets the polar point but is constant in time and uniform across the course; there is no pinching/footing trade-off, no wave-state effect, and no gust response.
  • Tack losses. Tacks are instantaneous and free — no speed loss or turning time.
  • Wind speed gradient / pressure differences. More breeze on one side of the course is not modelled.
  • Other boats. No dirty air, no right-of-way situations, no start-line traffic.
  • Waves, leeway, sea state. Leeway is implicitly folded into the tacking angle you enter; waves are ignored.
  • Start position and timing. Every run starts at the middle of the line at t = 0 — pin-end vs boat-end bias is visible in the geometry but not exploited by the simulated boat.
  • Time-varying current. The current field is frozen in time (no tidal cycle within a beat).

Practical reading: the tool answers "given this water and these three wind directions, which side is faster on pure geometry + current?" Treat the second list as the tactical layer you add on top of it.

Course

Mark bearing is the compass bearing from the middle of the start line. The chart is drawn course-up: the mark is always straight up, with the start line at the bottom. The start line is set square to the start→mark axis, so it stays horizontal on the chart regardless of wind direction.
